Relay Race

Teamwork in a single baton — the science of the 4x100m relay

Introduction

What Is a Relay Race?

The team event of track and field — 4 runners pass a baton while sprinting at top speed

  • 1
    The flower of team track — In a sport dominated by individual events, relay is the only one where teamwork determines the outcome. 4 runners pass a baton over a set distance.
  • 2
    4x100m relay — The signature event: each runner covers 100m for a total of 400m. The highlight of the Olympics and World Championships! Baton pass accuracy and speed are critical.
  • 3
    4x400m relay — Each runner covers 400m (one full lap). Requires not just sprint ability but also pacing and endurance management.

History

History of the Relay Race

From ancient torch relays to iconic Olympic moments

  • Ancient
    Origins in torch relay — The ancient Greek torch relay "Lampadedromia" is the ancestor of the relay race. A ritual of passing the sacred flame between altars evolved into a competition.
  • 1912
    Olympic official event — The 4x100m relay became an official event at the Stockholm Olympics. The 4x400m relay also began at the same Games.
  • 2012
    Jamaica's legend — Usain Bolt's Jamaica set the 4x100m world record of 36.84 seconds at the 2012 London Olympics! (The 2008 Beijing gold was stripped due to Nesta Carter's doping.)
  • 2020
    Mixed relay introduced — The mixed 4x400m relay became an official event at the Tokyo Olympics! 2 men and 2 women per team, promoting gender equality.
  • World
    World records — Men's 4x100m: Jamaica 36.84s (2012 London). Women's 4x100m: USA 40.82s (2012 London). Both set at the London Olympics.

The relay race is one of the most popular events at school sports festivals!

Rules

Relay Race Rules

Baton passing and lane keeping — essential rules to know

  • 1
    Takeover zone — Baton exchanges must occur within a 30m takeover zone. Exchanging outside the zone means disqualification! (Since 2018, the former 10m acceleration zone was merged into the 30m zone.)
  • 2
    Dropped baton — If the baton is dropped, the runner who dropped it must pick it up. They can continue as long as they don't interfere with other teams' lanes.
  • 3
    Lane keeping — In 4x100m, teams must stay in their assigned lanes. Lane violation means disqualification! In 4x400m, only the first runner keeps their lane; after that it is open.
  • 4
    Disqualification — Takeover zone violation, lane departure, interfering with another team, or failing to carry the baton to the finish all result in disqualification. The baton must be passed hand-to-hand.

Key Skills 1

Baton Pass

The most critical technique that decides relay victory or defeat

  • 1
    Upsweep pass — The giver sweeps the baton upward into the receiver's hand. The receiver faces palm down, catching between thumb and index finger. The most common technique in international competition!
  • 2
    Downsweep pass — The giver places the baton downward into the receiver's hand. The receiver extends palm up in a V-shape. Stable but requires a grip change.
  • 3
    Visual/non-visual pass — Visual: receiver looks back (suited for 4x400m). Non-visual: receiver extends hand behind without looking (essential for 4x100m). Non-visual results in less speed loss.
  • 4
    Left→right pattern — 1st runner right hand → 2nd left → 3rd right → 4th left. Alternating minimizes collision risk on curves.

A single baton pass can save 0.3-0.5 seconds. Over 3 exchanges, that is up to 1.5 seconds — enough to decide a medal!

Key Skills 2

Runner Roles by Leg

4 runners, each with different roles and strengths

  • 1st
    Starter — Uses a crouch start and must handle the curve, so curve-running ability is crucial. Holds the baton in the right hand and hugs the inside of the lane.
  • 2nd
    Curve specialist — Runs the curve while receiving the baton in the left hand and switching to the right. Maintaining speed on curves and accurate baton reception are key.
  • 3rd
    Curve-to-straight connector — Starts on the curve and enters the straight. Begins accelerating on the curve to reach top speed on the straight; a runner with great acceleration is ideal.
  • 4th
    Anchor — The team's final runner and ace! Sprints the straight and decides the outcome in close finishes. Typically the team's fastest runner.

Runner placement is a coaching strategy! The fastest runner doesn't always anchor. Curve ability, start reaction, and baton pass proficiency are all considered.

Key Skills 3

Check Mark and Timing

A single mark determines the success of the baton pass

  • 1
    Setting the check mark — A reference point on the track that the receiving runner uses to judge when to start. When the incoming runner reaches this mark, the receiver begins running.
  • 2
    Measuring distance — Typically placed about 7-10m behind the start of the takeover zone. Adjusted based on speed differences between runners, optimized through practice.
  • 3
    Timing practice — Commands like "Go!" or "Hand!" signal the baton request. When the giver calls, the receiver extends their hand behind without looking — this must be drilled repeatedly.
  • 4
    Pass at top speed — The ideal baton pass occurs when both runners are near top speed. Starting too early means decelerating at the zone's end; too late means failing to complete the exchange.

Check mark positioning must be adjusted each time based on weather, condition, and track surface. Always verify during warm-ups before a competition!

Tactics

Relay Tactics

Team strategy and leg management — as important as the baton pass

🏃 Runner Placement

1st leg: fast reaction and good curve runner. 2nd/3rd: accurate baton passers. Anchor: fastest or most competitive runner.

📐 Zone Utilization

Passing early in the zone gives the receiver less acceleration distance. Passing in the back half allows both runners to be near top speed — aim for the back of the zone!

🔄 Curve Technique

On curves, lean inward and swing the outside arm wide. Push harder with the left foot to counter centrifugal force, and run the inside edge for the shortest path.

🤝 Team Coordination

Baton pass success rate is proportional to practice volume. Drill with the same partner to internalize check mark placement, timing, and verbal cues. At least 30 practice passes are recommended.

Safety Guidelines

Playing Safely

Safety first when sprinting at top speed in relays

⚠️ Collision During Baton Pass

Watch for collisions when exchanging the baton. Pre-determine inside/outside running positions for giver and receiver to prevent crashes.

⚠️ Lane Departure

Leaving your lane risks collision with other teams. Be especially careful not to drift outward on curves. After the finish, slow down gradually instead of stopping abruptly.

⚠️ Warm-up Required

Relays involve all-out sprinting, so muscle injury risk (hamstrings, calves) is high. Always jog and do dynamic stretching for 10+ minutes, then do 2-3 acceleration runs at 50-80% effort.

⚠️ Eye Direction

Looking back during non-visual passes slows you down and increases lane departure and collision risks. Receivers must always look forward and extend their hand behind — practice this thoroughly.

Practice Activities

Try It in Class

Step-by-step practice to master baton passing and teamwork

  • 1
    Stationary baton pass — 2 people stand side by side exchanging the baton. Repeat to internalize hand position, grip, and verbal cues. Practice both upsweep and downsweep.
  • 2
    Walking pass — Exchange the baton while walking slowly. The receiver practices non-visual passing: looking forward and extending hand behind.
  • 3
    Jogging pass — Lightly jog and pass the baton within the takeover zone. This stage focuses on setting check marks and matching timing.
  • 4
    Full-speed pass — Pass the baton at 80-100% speed in race-like conditions. Fine-tune check mark placement for optimal timing.
  • 5
    Full 4-person relay — Run an actual 4x100m as a team of 4. Connect all 3 baton passes to synchronize the entire team's rhythm.

Start slow for accuracy first, then gradually increase speed. Don't get discouraged by drops — just keep practicing! Practice is skill.

Quiz

True/False Quiz

Let's check what you've learned!

  • Q1
    The takeover zone is 20m long. — False. Since the 2018 rule change merging the acceleration zone, it is 30m.
  • Q2
    Dropping the baton means immediate disqualification. — False. The runner who dropped it can pick it up and continue (disqualified only if interfering with other lanes).
  • Q3
    In 4x100m, non-visual passes result in less speed loss. — True. The receiver looks forward while running, which is better for maintaining speed.
  • Q4
    In 4x100m, all runners must stay in their assigned lane. — True. 4x100m uses separate lanes; leaving your lane means disqualification.
  • Q5
    The anchor (4th runner) must always be the team's fastest member. — False. Curve ability, start reaction, and baton pass proficiency are all considered in placement.
