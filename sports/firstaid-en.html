First Aid

Emergency response skills every PE student should know

Introduction

What Is First Aid?

Immediate care given at the scene before professional medical help arrives

  • 1
    Definition — First Aid is the basic treatment provided immediately at the scene when an injury or acute illness occurs, before professional medical personnel arrive.
  • 2
    Why Is It Important? — Appropriate first aid within the Golden Time can prevent injuries from worsening, speed up recovery, and in severe cases, save lives.
  • 3
    First Aid in PE Class — During exercise, various situations can arise including muscle/joint injuries, heat-related illness, and bleeding. Basic first aid knowledge is essential for every student.
  • 4
    Three Principles of First Aid — (1) Check scene safety (protect yourself first) (2) Call 119 (request professional help) (3) Treat within your ability (don't overextend). Always remember these three!
On-scene treatment before professionals arrive Act within the Golden Time Your own safety comes first
Muscle & Joint Injuries

RICE Protocol

Basic first aid for muscle and joint injuries — Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation

  • R
    Rest — Stop moving the injured area immediately. Continuing to exercise worsens the damage and extends recovery time. "Just a little more" is off limits! Stop activity immediately and find a comfortable position.
  • I
    Ice — Apply an ice bag or cold pack to the injury for 15–20 minutes. Cold constricts blood vessels, reducing swelling and pain. Always place a towel between ice and skin to prevent frostbite. Repeat every 2 hours.
  • C
    Compression — Wrap the injured area with an elastic bandage at moderate pressure. This helps control swelling. If fingers become numb or cold, the wrap is too tight — loosen it immediately.
  • E
    Elevation — Raise the injured area above the level of the heart. Gravity helps reduce swelling and improves circulation. For ankle injuries, rest the leg on pillows or cushions.
Rest Ice 15–20 min Compression Elevation

RICE is most effective within 48–72 hours of injury. The sooner you apply it, the faster the recovery!

Muscle Cramps

Muscle Cramps

Sudden muscle contractions with intense pain — causes and treatment

  • 1
    Causes — (1) Dehydration: insufficient fluid impairs muscle function (2) Electrolyte imbalance: minerals like sodium, potassium, and magnesium (3) Muscle overuse: exercise intensity beyond your normal level (4) Inadequate warm-up: suddenly loading cold muscles.
  • 2
    Treatment — (1) Stop exercising immediately (2) Slowly stretch the cramping muscle in the opposite direction (for calf cramps, pull the toes toward you) (3) Gently massage the area (4) Drink a small amount of water or sports drink.
  • 3
    Prevention — (1) Hydrate adequately before, during, and after exercise (150–200 mL every 15–20 min during exercise) (2) Warm up and cool down thoroughly (3) Maintain balanced nutrition for mineral intake (4) Increase exercise intensity gradually.
Stretch slowly to release Hydrate + Replenish electrolytes Prevention is best!
Joint Injury

Ankle Sprain

The most common PE injury — what to do when you twist your ankle

  • 1
    Symptoms — (1) Swelling around the ankle develops rapidly (2) Severe pain when bearing weight (3) Bruising (subcutaneous bleeding) appears over time (4) In severe cases, the joint may feel unstable and difficult to move.
  • 2
    Apply RICE — (1) Rest: stop activity immediately, avoid bearing weight (2) Ice: apply an ice pack wrapped in a towel for 15–20 min (3) Compression: wrap in a figure-8 pattern with elastic bandage (4) Elevation: raise the leg above heart level.
  • 3
    Basic Taping — For ankle stability, apply non-elastic tape from the outer to inner side to support the ligament. Incorrect taping can impede circulation, so only do it after proper instruction.
  • 4
    When to See a Doctor — (1) Cannot bear any weight at all (2) Swelling progresses significantly (3) Severe tenderness when pressing on the bone (4) No improvement after 48 hours → Possible fracture; get an X-ray at the hospital.
Most common PE injury Apply RICE immediately No improvement? See a doctor
Heat-Related Illness

Heat Stroke / Heat Exhaustion

The most dangerous threat during summer outdoor PE — learn to distinguish heat exhaustion from heat stroke

  • 1
    Heat Exhaustion — Body temperature 38–40°C. Conscious but dizzy, nauseous, with headache, heavy sweating, and cool/clammy skin. Moving to shade and hydrating usually leads to recovery.
  • 2
    Heat Stroke — Body temperature 40°C or higher! Confused or altered consciousness. No sweating; skin is hot and dry. This is a life-threatening emergency — call 119 immediately.
  • 3
    Treatment — (1) Move to a cool, shaded area immediately (2) Loosen clothing (3) Cool the body with wet towels or fanning (4) If conscious, give small sips of cool water (5) If unconscious, NEVER give fluids — place in recovery position.
  • 4
    When to Call 119 — (1) Temperature 40°C+ (2) Confused or slurred speech (3) Severe vomiting (4) Seizures. Heat stroke has a high mortality rate — never hesitate!
Heat Exhaustion Conscious, 38–40°C, heavy sweating Heat Stroke Confused, 40°C+, no sweating Heat stroke = Call 119 immediately
Bleeding

Nosebleed

When you get a sudden nosebleed during exercise — proper treatment and common myths

  • 1
    Proper Treatment — (1) Lean your head slightly forward (NOT backward!) (2) Pinch both sides of the nostrils firmly with thumb and index finger (3) Hold for 10 continuous minutes (4) Breathe through your mouth and stay calm.
  • 2
    Common Myth — "Tilt your head back" is WRONG! Blood can flow down the throat causing nausea or vomiting, and may enter the airway. Always lean forward.
  • 3
    Additional Measures — Applying a cold pack to the bridge of the nose or back of the neck can help constrict blood vessels. Stuffing tissue into the nose is not recommended as removal can cause re-bleeding.
  • 4
    When to See a Doctor — (1) Bleeding doesn't stop after 20+ minutes (2) Nosebleeds occur frequently (3) Nosebleed after a head impact (4) Extremely heavy bleeding → Medical attention needed.
Lean head forward Pinch nostrils for 10 minutes Tilting head back is wrong!
Fracture & Dislocation

Suspected Fracture / Dislocation

When a bone may be broken or a joint dislocated — on-scene response

  • 1
    Fracture Signs — Extreme pain, visible deformity, abnormal movement, severe swelling and bruising. If you heard a "snap," fracture is highly likely.
  • 2
    Do Not Move! — Do not move the injured area. Improper movement can cause additional damage to surrounding nerves, blood vessels, and muscles. Keep the most comfortable position.
  • 3
    Improvised Splinting — Use a rigid object nearby (board, magazine, umbrella) to immobilize the joints on either side of the injury. Place cloth between the splint and skin to reduce pressure. Without a splint, bind the injured arm to the body or the injured leg to the healthy leg.
  • 4
    Ice + Call 119 — Apply ice to reduce swelling and call 119 immediately.
  • 5
    NEVER Attempt to Reset! — Never try to push a dislocated joint back in yourself! This can cause serious secondary damage to bones, ligaments, and nerves. Only medical professionals should treat this.
Do not move Splint to immobilize NEVER reset it yourself Call 119 immediately
Wounds & Bleeding

Wounds & Bleeding

From scrapes to heavy bleeding — treatment by situation

  • 1
    Abrasion (Scrape) — (1) Rinse the wound thoroughly under clean running water (remove debris) (2) Apply antiseptic (3) Cover with a clean gauze pad or bandage to prevent secondary infection. Even minor scrapes from falling need proper infection prevention.
  • 2
    Laceration (Cut) — (1) Press a clean gauze or cloth directly on the wound (direct pressure) (2) Maintain pressure for 5–10 minutes (3) Once bleeding stops, disinfect and cover with gauze (4) Deep or long cuts may need stitches — go to the hospital.
  • 3
    Heavy Bleeding — (1) Call 119 immediately (2) Apply strong direct pressure with a clean cloth (3) If blood soaks through, do NOT remove the cloth — add more on top and keep pressing (4) Elevate the bleeding area above the heart if possible.
Abrasion Rinse → Disinfect → Cover Laceration Direct pressure 5–10 min Heavy bleeding 119 + Direct pressure
Bee Stings & Allergies

Bee Stings & Allergic Reactions

What to do when stung by a bee during outdoor PE — be prepared for anaphylaxis

  • 1
    Removing the Stinger — If the stinger is embedded, scrape it out with a flat object like a credit card. Using tweezers or fingers can squeeze the venom sac and inject more venom.
  • 2
    Basic Treatment — (1) Wash the sting site with soap and water (2) Apply cold to reduce swelling and pain (3) Avoid scratching the area.
  • 3
    Signs of Anaphylaxis — If the following symptoms appear after a sting, it is a severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis): (1) Difficulty breathing, wheezing (2) Swelling of lips, tongue, or throat (3) Widespread hives (4) Dizziness, loss of consciousness (5) Sudden drop in blood pressure.
  • 4
    EpiPen + 119 — If anaphylaxis is suspected: (1) If an EpiPen (epinephrine auto-injector) is available, inject it immediately into the outer thigh (2) Call 119 (3) Lay the patient down and elevate their legs. Hospital care is still required even after using an EpiPen.
Scrape stinger out Breathing difficulty + Swelling = Anaphylaxis EpiPen + 119 immediately
Safety Rules

First Aid Safety Rules

Fundamental principles to follow when administering first aid

🛡 Your Safety First

Check scene safety before helping. In dangerous environments (fire, electrocution, traffic), your own safety is the top priority. Preventing secondary accidents enables you to help more.

🧤 Wear Gloves

Always wear disposable gloves when treating bleeding patients. No gloves? Wrap your hands in a plastic bag. This is a crucial step to prevent bloodborne infections (Hepatitis B, HIV, etc.).

🚫 Don't Move the Patient Carelessly

If a spinal (neck/back) injury is suspected, NEVER move the patient. Improper movement can cause spinal cord damage leading to permanent paralysis. Wait for professional rescue.

📞 Call 119 First

For serious injuries or loss of consciousness, calling 119 comes before treatment. Report the location, number of patients, and their condition concisely. Don't hang up first — follow the dispatcher's instructions.

Quiz

True / False Quiz

Let's check what you have learned!

  • Q1
    When you have a nosebleed, you should tilt your head backward. — False. Tilting backward causes blood to flow down the throat, which is dangerous. Lean forward and pinch your nostrils for 10 minutes.
  • Q2
    In RICE, the "I" stands for Ice (cold application). — True. Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation.
  • Q3
    A dislocated joint should be pushed back into place at the scene. — False. Never attempt to reset it! There is a risk of nerve and blood vessel damage, so only medical professionals should treat this.
  • Q4
    Heat stroke is an emergency with a body temperature above 40°C and altered consciousness. — True. Heat stroke is a life-threatening condition requiring an immediate call to 119.
  • Q5
    When blood soaks through the gauze during bleeding, you should remove it and replace it with new gauze. — False. Removing the gauze dislodges the forming blood clot and worsens bleeding. Add new gauze on top and continue pressing.
