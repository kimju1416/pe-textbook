Long Jump

Turning running speed into flight — the science of the leap

Introduction

What Is Long Jump?

A track and field event where you sprint, take off from a board, and jump as far as possible into a sand pit

  • 1
    Objective — Sprint, precisely hit the takeoff board, leap, and land as far as possible in the sand pit
  • 2
    Phases — Approach run → Takeoff → Flight → Landing — 4 phases make up the jump
  • 3
    Measurement — Distance is measured from the front edge of the takeoff board to the nearest mark left in the sand
Oldest track event Has existed since the ancient Olympics A blend of running speed + jumping power + technique Middle school averages: Boys 3.5-4.5m · Girls 2.8-3.8m
History

History of the Long Jump

From ancient hand weights to the modern 8.95m — the record of human leaps

  • BC 708
    Ancient Olympics — Included in the ancient Greek pentathlon. Athletes jumped holding stone weights called "halteres" in both hands for added momentum!
  • 1896
    Modern Olympic official event — Men's long jump became official at the 1st Athens Olympics. The first gold medal distance was 6.35m.
  • 1936
    The legend of Jesse Owens — Won gold at the Berlin Olympics with 8.06m. A magnificent leap that transcended racial discrimination.
  • 1968
    Bob Beamon's leap of the century — 8.90m at the Mexico City Olympics! Surpassing the previous record by 55cm, this extraordinary mark stood for 23 years.
  • 1991
    Mike Powell's world record — 8.95m at the Tokyo World Championships, a record that still stands today!

Records indicate that ancient Olympic athletes jumped to the rhythm of flute music to find their tempo!

Rules

Competition Rules

Know the rules for a clean jump and a valid record

  • 1
    Takeoff board — A white wooden board 20cm wide, 1.22m long. You must take off from this board. A plasticine (clay) indicator is attached to the front edge — stepping on it leaves a mark indicating a foul.
  • 2
    Foul line — Stepping past the front edge of the takeoff board is a foul! Even if your toe barely crosses, the jump is invalid. A mark in the plasticine confirms the foul.
  • 3
    Distance measurement — Measured at right angles from the front edge of the board to the nearest mark in the sand made by any body part. If your hips or hands touch behind your feet, the measurement is taken from there!
  • 4
    Number of attempts — 3 qualifying attempts; top 8 advance to finals with 3 more. School PE typically allows 3-6 attempts.
  • 5
    Wind regulation — If tailwind exceeds 2.0 m/s, the record is not ratified. Too much wind assistance is deemed unfair!
Approach distance Max 40-45m (set freely on the runway) Sand pit Min 6m long, 2.75-3m wide Time limit 1 minute per attempt
Key Skills 1

Approach and Takeoff

The critical moment of converting maximum speed into leap energy

  • 1
    Approach run — Start slow, gradually accelerate to reach top speed just before the board! Typically 12-18 strides. Consistent stride length and rhythm are key.
  • 2
    Hitting the board precisely — Hit as close to the front as possible without crossing. Strike firmly with the whole foot! Quick contact with the full sole (or ball of the foot) transfers power.
  • 3
    Last 3-step rhythm — "Ta-ta-ta!" A rhythmic final 3 steps are the key to success. Make the second-to-last step slightly longer and the last step short and quick to lift your center of gravity.
  • 4
    Arm action — At takeoff, drive the opposite arm and knee powerfully upward to increase jump height. Swing both arms from back to front in a big motion!
Key Skills 2

Flight Position

Three techniques for maintaining balance in the air

  • 1
    Sail — Recommended for beginners! Simply extend both legs forward in the air. Easy to learn and commonly used in school PE.
  • 2
    Hang — Arch the body backward in the air, then tuck the legs forward just before landing. The longer hang time allows for greater distance.
  • 3
    Hitch-kick — An advanced technique of "walking" 2-3 steps in the air with alternating legs! Counteracts forward rotation for a better landing position. Used by elite athletes.
  • 4
    In-flight balance — Regardless of technique, the key is suppressing forward rotation! If the upper body pitches forward, landing distance decreases.
Key Skills 3

Landing

The final moment determines your record!

  • 1
    Both feet forward — Just before landing, lift both legs as far forward and high as possible. The further your toes reach, the longer the record.
  • 2
    Watch the hips — If your hips fall backward on landing, that mark becomes the measurement point, drastically reducing your record! Lean the upper body forward to shift weight ahead.
  • 3
    Arm action — Reach both arms far forward to pull the upper body ahead. At the moment of landing, sweep arms downward while folding the body forward!
  • 4
    Bend the knees — Bend the knees on impact to absorb the shock and let the body fall forward. Never fall backward!
Tactics

Strategies for Better Records

Combining technique and strategy can add inches to your jump

👟 Approach Stride Adjustment

Finding your exact starting position is first! Through practice, set your stride count and use a check mark to consistently hit the board with the same foot.

⚖️ Accuracy vs Speed

Slowing down for accuracy reduces distance; going too fast increases foul risk. Practice finding the balance point between top speed and precision!

🌬️ Using the Wind

Tailwind days increase approach speed and are advantageous. In headwinds, try slightly raising the takeoff angle to extend hang time.

📐 Optimal Takeoff Angle

Theoretically 45° is optimal, but in practice 18-25° is most effective due to running speed. Jumping too high reduces horizontal distance.

🧠 Mental Strategy

Post a safe, solid mark on your first attempt, then go bold on remaining tries. Overcoming fear of fouling is also important!

💪 Physical Training

Sprint training (speed) + squats/lunges (leg strength) + plyometrics (explosiveness) will improve both approach speed and takeoff power.

Safety Guidelines

Playing Safely

Staying injury-free is the most important thing!

🏖️ Check the Sand Pit

Always level the sand and remove rocks, glass, and debris before jumping. Hard sand increases impact injuries.

🦵 Ankle/Knee Injuries

Great force is applied to ankles at takeoff and knees at landing. Use ankle tape or braces, and stop immediately if your knee hurts.

🤸 Landing Technique Is Key

Poor landing form is the leading cause of back and hip injuries! Practice correct landing thoroughly — never fall backward or to the side.

🏃 Warm-up Required

Long jump uses explosive full-body effort. At least 10 minutes of jogging + dynamic stretching (leg swings, knee raises, ankle rotations) to warm up thoroughly.

Practice Activities

How to Practice at School

Step-by-step practice so anyone can enjoy long jump

  • 1
    Standing long jump — Jump from a standing position with both feet. A basic exercise to develop arm swing and landing sense.
  • 2
    3-step / 5-step long jump — Short approach (3-5 steps) to practice takeoff and leap sensation. Focus on accurate board contact!
  • 3
    Full approach practice — Set your stride count, place a check mark, and repeatedly practice hitting the board at full speed.
  • 4
    Landing practice — Jump from a low height to repeatedly train proper landing (feet forward, arms extended).
  • 5
    Record challenge — Measure your distance and challenge your personal best each class! Compete with friends and enjoy the sense of achievement.

Schools without sand pits can use gym mats or practice with standing long jump and triple jump variations!

Quiz

True/False Quiz

Let's check what you've learned!

  • Q1
    If any part of your foot crosses the front edge of the takeoff board, it is a foul. — True. A mark in the plasticine strip confirms a foul and the jump is invalidated.
  • Q2
    Distance is measured from where your feet land in the sand. — False. It is measured to the nearest body mark in the sand (including hips and hands).
  • Q3
    The actual takeoff angle for long jumpers is about 45 degrees. — False. While 45° is theoretically optimal, 18-25° is most effective in practice due to running speed.
  • Q4
    If tailwind exceeds 2.0 m/s, the record is not ratified. — True. Excessive wind assistance is deemed unfair.
  • Q5
    The "Sail" technique involves alternating legs in the air like walking. — False. The sail is the simplest beginner technique with both legs extended forward; the hitch-kick is the walking technique.
