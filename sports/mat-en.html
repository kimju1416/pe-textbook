Mat Exercises

Roll, stand, spin — discover what your body can do

Introduction

What Are Mat Exercises?

A foundational artistic gymnastics event involving rolls, handstands, rotations, and more — all performed on a mat

  • 1
    Definition — Exercises performed on a thick cushioned mat that develop balance, flexibility, and strength through rolling, inverting, and holding the body
  • 2
    Features — Performed using only your own body, with no equipment. Mat exercises are the starting point for all artistic gymnastics.
  • 3
    Benefits — Develops flexibility, strength, balance, spatial awareness, courage, and confidence all at once.

History

History of Mat Exercises

From ancient gymnastics to Olympic floor exercises

  • Ancient
    Greek Gymnastics — Ancient Greeks practiced rolls, handstands, and other movements for physical training. The word "Gymnastics" itself originates from the Greek language!
  • 19C
    German Gymnastics Systematized — Friedrich Jahn systematically organized gymnastics apparatus and movements. Known as the "Father of Gymnastics," he established the foundations of mat exercises.
  • 1932
    Olympic Floor Exercise — Men's floor exercise became an official Olympic event at the LA Olympics. Women's floor exercise was added in 1952.
  • Today
    Essential School PE Event — Taught as a foundational gymnastics event in school PE programs worldwide. In Korean middle schools, it is a key component of the "Challenge Activity" domain.

The Olympic floor exercise is performed on a 12 m x 12 m mat. Men perform for up to 70 seconds without music; women perform for up to 90 seconds with music. The basics you learn at school are the starting point for everything!

Basic Movements

Basic Mat Exercise Movements

Mat exercise movements are broadly classified into five categories

  • 1
    Rolls — Forward roll, backward roll, side roll. The most fundamental movements in mat exercises, performed by curling the body into a ball and rotating.
  • 2
    Stands / Balance — Handstand (tripod/freestanding), shoulder stand, headstand. Balancing while inverted.
  • 3
    Rotations — Cartwheel, handspring, back handspring. Advanced movements where the entire body rotates around the hands or feet.
  • 4
    Bridge — An arched position supporting the body on both hands and feet with the back curved backward. Requires flexibility and lower-back strength.
  • 5
    Jumps & Landings — Leaps and stable landings between connecting movements. A key element that elevates the overall quality of a routine.

Key Skill 1

Forward Roll · Backward Roll

The most fundamental mat skill — how to roll safely

  • 1
    Forward Roll Setup — Squat at the edge of the mat and place both hands shoulder-width apart on the mat. Tuck your chin firmly to your chest to protect your neck!
  • 2
    Forward Roll Execution — Roll forward touching the mat in this order: back of head, upper back, lower back. Round your back as much as possible to roll smoothly like a ball!
  • 3
    Backward Roll Setup — From a squat, place both hands beside your ears (palms facing up). Tuck your chin to your chest.
  • 4
    Backward Roll Execution — Sit back onto your hips, rolling in this order: back, then back of head. When your head touches the mat, push hard with both hands to keep your weight off your neck!

Key Points: Tuck your chin + Round your back + Keep weight off your neck. Follow these three rules and you can roll safely!

Key Skill 2

Handstand

The courage to see the world upside down — the pinnacle of balance

  • 1
    Tripod Headstand — Support yourself on the front of your head (forehead) and both hands — three points of contact. Form a triangle shape for a stable base.
  • 2
    Handstand (Two-point) — Support your entire body weight on just your hands and hold your body vertically. Use your fingers for fine balance adjustments.
  • 3
    Wall-Assisted Practice — Start by practicing against a wall! Place your hands about 30 cm from the wall and kick up one leg at a time to lean against it.
  • 4
    Finding Balance — Press your fingers into the floor to control forward-backward balance. Keep your eyes on the floor between your hands. Tighten your whole body into a straight line!

Safety Tip: Always practice with a spotter! If you start to fall, tuck your chin and exit into a forward roll.

Key Skill 3

Combination Moves · Applications

Link the basics together to create your own routine

  • 1
    Linking Moves — Perform basic movements seamlessly, such as forward roll → jump → backward roll. The smoother the flow, the higher the quality!
  • 2
    Cartwheel — Starting sideways, land in the sequence: hand → hand → foot → foot as you rotate sideways. Extend arms and legs fully to form a star shape!
  • 3
    Handspring (Introduction) — An advanced skill where you run, place your hands, flip your body over, and land. Middle schoolers should attempt this step by step with a spotter.
  • 4
    Create Your Own Routine — Link 3–5 movements to create your own mat exercise routine. Include a starting pose, movements, and a finishing pose!
Practice Methods

Effective Practice Methods

Consistent, step-by-step practice is the secret to improvement

📈 Progressive Practice

Start simple! Progress in this order: forward roll → backward roll → handstand → cartwheel → combination routines. Master the basics before moving to the next level!

🤝 Use a Spotter

Handstands, backward rolls, etc. must always be done with a partner! The spotter supports your waist or back and catches you if you fall. Take turns helping each other.

🧘 Stretching Is Essential

Flexibility is key in mat exercises! Before every class, stretch your neck, shoulders, waist, wrists, and legs for at least 10 minutes to stay injury-free and move smoothly.

💪 Overcoming Fear

Feeling scared of rolling backward or standing upside down is perfectly natural! Start at low heights and use support equipment (wedge mats, incline mats) to ease your anxiety.

Safety Rules

Staying Safe

Safety is the top priority in mat exercises — follow the rules!

🧹 Check Mat Condition

Make sure mats are thick enough, have no gaps at the seams, and are non-slip. Never use mats that are worn or torn.

🦴 Neck Protection

The neck is the most vulnerable during rolls! Always tuck your chin to your chest and never let your weight rest directly on your neck. Neck-bending movements are strictly forbidden.

🤝 Spotter Required

Challenging movements like handstands, backward rolls, and cartwheels must always be done with a spotter! Never try a new skill alone.

🏃 Warm Up for 10+ Minutes

Thoroughly loosen your neck, shoulders, waist, wrists, and ankles. Neck circles and wrist stretches are a must! Start only after your body feels warm.

⛔ No Reckless Skill Attempts

Attempting advanced skills (handspring, back handspring, etc.) without mastering the basics can lead to serious injuries. Progress step by step with your teacher's permission!

Quiz

True / False Quiz

Let's check what you have learned!

  • Q1
    The most important thing in a forward roll is tucking your chin to your chest. — True. Tucking your chin protects your neck and rounds your back, enabling a safe roll.
  • Q2
    During a backward roll, you should push the floor with both hands when the back of your head touches the mat. — True. Pushing hard with both hands keeps your weight off your head — the key to safety.
  • Q3
    It is safe to practice handstands alone. — False. You must always practice with a spotter, and you should also practice exiting into a forward roll if you fall.
  • Q4
    In a cartwheel, you land in the order: hand → hand → foot → foot. — True. Extend your arms and legs fully to form a star shape as you rotate sideways.
  • Q5
    In the Olympics, men's floor exercise is performed with music. — False. Men perform without music for up to 70 seconds; only women perform with music for up to 90 seconds.
