CPR

The 4-minute miracle — anyone can save a life

Introduction

What Is CPR?

An emergency procedure to revive a person whose heart has stopped — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation

  • 1
    CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) — An emergency procedure that artificially circulates blood and maintains breathing for a patient in cardiac arrest (sudden heart stoppage).
  • 2
    Golden Time: 4 Minutes — Starting CPR within 4 minutes of cardiac arrest doubles or triples the survival rate. After 4 minutes, brain cells begin to suffer damage.
  • 3
    Anyone Can Do It — You do not need to be a medical professional to perform CPR. Even chest compressions alone can significantly improve survival rates!

Recognition

Recognizing Cardiac Arrest

What to do first when you find someone who has collapsed

  • 1
    Check Consciousness — Tap both shoulders and ask loudly, "Are you okay?" If there is no response, suspect cardiac arrest.
  • 2
    Check Breathing (10 seconds) — Observe the chest and abdomen for movement for 10 seconds. If the person is not breathing or breathing abnormally (gasping), it is cardiac arrest.
  • 3
    Call 119 (Emergency Services) — Point to a specific bystander and say, "Please call 119!" If alone, call 119 on speakerphone.
  • 4
    Request an AED — Point to another person and say, "Please bring the AED (Automated External Defibrillator)!" Most public places have AEDs available.

Important! When asking bystanders for help, be specific — "You in the red shirt!" A vague "Someone please help" often results in no one acting.

Core Technique

Chest Compressions

The most critical part of CPR! Accurate chest compressions circulate the blood

  • 1
    Correct Position — Place the heel of one hand on the center of the sternum (breastbone), between the nipples. Interlock your other hand on top.
  • 2
    Depth: About 5 cm — Compress the chest approximately 5 cm (max 6 cm). Too shallow is ineffective; too deep risks injury.
  • 3
    Rate: 100–120 per minute — About the tempo of "Baby Shark" or "Stayin' Alive."
  • 4
    Proper Posture — Keep your elbows straight and position your shoulders directly over the patient's chest. Use your body weight, not arm strength, to compress.
  • 5
    30 Consecutive Compressions — Perform 30 compressions without interruption. Allow the chest to fully recoil (release) between compressions.
Position Center of sternum, between nipples Depth ~5 cm Rate 100–120/min Count 30 consecutive
Core Technique

Rescue Breaths

After 30 chest compressions, give 2 rescue breaths — the 30:2 ratio

  • 1
    Head Tilt–Chin Lift — Tilt the forehead back with one hand, and lift the bony part of the chin with the other to open the airway.
  • 2
    Pinch the Nose — Use the thumb and index finger of the hand on the forehead to pinch the patient's nose shut.
  • 3
    Seal and Blow — Completely cover the patient's mouth with yours and blow for about 1 second. Ensure a tight seal so no air escapes.
  • 4
    Watch the Chest Rise — Confirm visually that the patient's chest rises when you blow. If it doesn't rise, reposition the airway.
  • 5
    Repeat After 2 Breaths — After 2 rescue breaths, resume chest compressions for another 30. Maintain the 30:2 ratio.

If rescue breaths are difficult or you are concerned about infection, continuous chest compressions alone are still highly effective!

Full Sequence

Full CPR Sequence

From discovering a cardiac arrest patient to the arrival of paramedics — the complete CPR procedure at a glance

  • ①
    Check Consciousness — Tap shoulders + Shout "Are you okay?"
  • ②
    Call 119 + Request AED — Point to specific bystanders to call 119 and fetch the AED
  • ③
    30 Chest Compressions — Center of sternum, 5 cm deep, 100–120/min — hard and fast
  • ④
    2 Rescue Breaths — Head tilt–chin lift to open airway, blow for 1 second each, 2 times
  • ⑤
    Repeat 30:2 — 30 compressions + 2 breaths until paramedics arrive
  • ⑥
    Use AED When Available — Turn on the AED immediately and follow the voice prompts
Key Hard, fast, and uninterrupted! Pause in compressions must be under 10 seconds Never stop until 119 arrives
AED

AED (Automated External Defibrillator) Usage

AEDs guide you with voice prompts — anyone can use one

  • ①
    Power On — Open the AED lid and press the power button. Voice instructions will begin.
  • ②
    Attach Pads — Remove the patient's clothing and attach the two pads: one below the right collarbone, one on the left side below the armpit (below the nipple).
  • ③
    Hands Off During Analysis — When the AED says "Analyzing," remove your hands from the patient and stand back. Movement interferes with accurate analysis.
  • ④
    Press the Shock Button — If the AED says "Shock advised," ensure everyone stands clear, then press the flashing shock button.
  • ⑤
    Resume CPR Immediately — Resume chest compressions immediately after the shock. The AED will automatically re-analyze every 2 minutes.

AEDs are required in public places such as airports, subway stations, schools, and government buildings. Make a habit of noting AED locations around you!

Choking

Heimlich Maneuver (Choking Response)

Emergency procedure when food or an object blocks the airway

  • 1
    Recognizing Choking — The person suddenly cannot speak or cough and clutches their throat (the universal choking sign).
  • 2
    5 Back Blows — Support the person's chest with one hand and deliver 5 firm strikes between the shoulder blades with the heel of your other hand.
  • 3
    5 Abdominal Thrusts — Stand behind the person, wrap your arms around them, place your fist between the navel and breastbone, and thrust firmly upward. Repeat 5 times.
  • 4
    Alternate and Repeat — Alternate between 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until the object is dislodged.
  • 5
    If Unconscious, Start CPR — If the person loses consciousness, lay them down and begin chest compressions (CPR) immediately. Call 119 as well.
Special Situations

CPR in Special Situations

CPR methods may vary depending on the patient's circumstances

  • 1
    Infant CPR (Under 1 Year) — Use 2 fingers (index + middle) for chest compressions. Depth is about 4 cm; rate is the same at 100–120/min. Compress just below the line connecting the nipples.
  • 2
    Pregnant Patient CPR — Chest compressions only as a rule. Place a wedge under the right hip to tilt the patient 15–30 degrees to the left, preventing the uterus from compressing the inferior vena cava.
  • 3
    Drowning Victim — Since breathing stops first, give 2 rescue breaths before starting chest compressions. Perform CPR on a firm surface after rescuing the person from water.

Safety Rules

CPR Safety Rules

Save a life, but always protect your own safety too

🛡️ Infection Prevention

Use a pocket mask or face shield if available. If not, chest compressions alone are fine. Minimize contact with blood and bodily fluids.

⚠️ Your Safety First

Check that the scene is safe before approaching. At traffic accidents, fires, or electrocution sites, only approach if it is safe for you.

🔄 Switch Rescuers (Every 2 min)

Chest compressions are physically demanding. Switch with another rescuer every 2 minutes (about 5 cycles). Fatigue reduces compression quality.

🚑 Don't Stop Until 119 Arrives

Do not stop CPR until paramedics arrive, the patient begins to move, or the AED instructs you to stop.

Legal Knowledge

Good Samaritan Law

Good-faith emergency care is protected by law — do not hesitate!

  • 1
    Emergency Medical Services Act, Article 5-2 — "A person who provides emergency care to a patient in a life-threatening situation shall not bear civil or criminal liability for property damage or personal injury resulting therefrom, unless there is willful misconduct or gross negligence."
  • 2
    Good-Faith Rescue — Emergency care performed with good intentions is legally protected even if the outcome is unfavorable. You are not held liable even if a rib fractures during CPR.
  • 3
    Inaction Is More Dangerous — Without CPR, the survival rate for cardiac arrest patients is less than 5%. Even imperfect CPR is far better than doing nothing at all.

Don't worry about breaking ribs. Rib fractures during proper CPR are a natural occurrence, and no rib is more important than a life!

Quiz

True / False Quiz

Let's check what you have learned!

  • Q1
    Chest compressions should be performed at over 200 per minute for best results. — False. The proper rate is 100–120 per minute. Going too fast prevents adequate chest recoil.
  • Q2
    Only medical professionals can use an AED. — False. AEDs are designed with voice prompts so that anyone can use them. Laypeople can and should use them.
  • Q3
    The ratio of chest compressions to rescue breaths is 30:2. — True. For adults, perform 30 chest compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths, and repeat.
  • Q4
    CPR must be started within 4 minutes of cardiac arrest to minimize brain damage. — True. After 4 minutes, brain cells begin to sustain irreversible damage, hence the 4-minute golden time.
  • Q5
    If you break a patient's rib while performing CPR in good faith, you will be legally punished. — False. The Good Samaritan law protects good-faith emergency care from legal liability.
