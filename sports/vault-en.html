Vault

A split second in the air — the art of takeoff and landing

Introduction

What Is Vault?

An artistic gymnastics event consisting of five phases: approach run, hurdle, contact, flight, and landing

  • 1
    Objective — To clear a vaulting horse safely and gracefully, then stick a stable landing
  • 2
    Classification — A fundamental event in artistic gymnastics; at the Olympics it is officially called "Vault"
  • 3
    Features — Requires a wide range of physical abilities including sprinting speed, jumping power, flexibility, aerial awareness, and landing stability
School PE Essential Core event in the middle-school challenge activity domain Approach → Hurdle → Contact → Flight → Landing A signature exercise that builds courage and a spirit of challenge
History

History of the Vault

From military training to the Olympic stage

  • 1811
    Father of German Gymnastics — Friedrich Jahn opened an outdoor gymnasium near Berlin and began using vault-like apparatus for military training.
  • 1896
    First Modern Olympics — Artistic gymnastics became an official sport at the Athens Olympics. The vault was included in the competition program.
  • 1950~
    Modern Vault Evolution — The traditional side horse was replaced by a lengthwise (tongue-shaped) vaulting table. Techniques advanced to include aerial rotations and twists.
  • 2001
    New Vaulting Table — The International Gymnastics Federation (FIG) officially adopted a safer vaulting table design, still in use today.
  • Today
    School PE in Korea — Vault is a core event in the "Challenge Activity" domain of the middle and high school PE curriculum. Adjustable height settings make it accessible to everyone.

Jahn's philosophy of gymnastics: "A healthy mind dwells in a healthy body!" The vault is also a symbol of courage and perseverance.

Rules · Competition

Competition Rules and Methods

Basic vault rules and methods every student should know

📏 Vault Height

In school classes, the vault is usually set at 4–6 levels (about 80–120 cm). Height is adjusted step by step to match each individual's level; the principle is never to raise it beyond your ability.

🏃 Approach Distance

An approach run of 15–25 m is typically used. Too short means insufficient speed; too far wastes energy. Finding the right distance for you is key!

🦶 Springboard Use

A Reuther board (springboard) is placed in front of the vault. Jump off it with both feet together. The gap between the board and the vault is usually 60–80 cm. A two-foot takeoff is standard.

🤸 Stable Landing

On landing, keep both feet together and bend your knees slightly to absorb the impact. Spread your arms to maintain balance and avoid falling backward or slipping.

Key Skill 1

Straddle Vault

The most fundamental vault technique — the first skill every student learns

  • 1
    Approach Run — Gradually increase speed. In the last 3–4 steps, adjust your rhythm to land precisely on the springboard.
  • 2
    Hurdle — Bring both feet together and push off the Reuther board hard! Strike with the balls of your feet for maximum spring.
  • 3
    Contact & Clear — Place both hands on the far end of the vault (contact), then spread your legs wide to clear the vault on either side. Pushing off the vault forcefully with your hands is the key!
  • 4
    Landing — Bring both feet together for a soft landing. Bend your knees to absorb the impact and spread your arms for balance.

Practice Tip: Start without a vault — just practice placing your hands on a mat and straddling over!

Key Skill 2

Forward Roll Vault

An intermediate technique — roll forward after contact, keeping your back rounded and neck safe

  • 1
    Approach & Hurdle — Same as the basic vault: build speed and push off the Reuther board with both feet.
  • 2
    Contact & Tuck — After placing both hands on the vault, tuck your chin toward your chest and round your back. Relaxing your neck and curving your spine is essential!
  • 3
    Roll — Roll across the vault with your back rounded. The sequence should flow naturally: back of the head, shoulders, then upper back.
  • 4
    Landing — After completing the roll, bring your feet together and land on the mat in front of the vault.

Caution: Neck protection is paramount! Always tuck your chin to your chest; a spotter must support your back and waist.

Key Skill 3

Headspring · Handspring

Advanced techniques using arm rebound after contact

  • 1
    Headspring — Place your forehead and both hands on the vault, then arch your back like a bow and spring up using the rebound from your arms and neck.
  • 2
    Handspring — Place only your hands on the vault and push off forcefully to propel your entire body into the air. This is the foundation of Olympic vault skills!
  • 3
    Key Point — At the moment of contact, open your shoulders and fully extend your arms to push off! Without a strong push, you cannot achieve sufficient height.
  • 4
    Precaution — Only attempt these after mastering the basics (straddle vault, forward roll). A spotter and thick mats are essential!
Practice Methods

Step-by-Step Practice

Start low and progress gradually — safe, systematic practice is the shortest path to improvement

🥇 Stage 1: Mat Practice

Without a vault, practice hand placement and straddle clearance or forward rolls on mats. The goal is to develop contact awareness and flight posture.

🥈 Stage 2: Low Height (3–4 levels)

Set the vault low and practice the full sequence. Focus on springboard positioning, contact timing, and landing balance.

🥉 Stage 3: Higher (5–6 levels)

Once confident, raise the vault one level at a time. Slightly increase your approach speed and push off harder at contact.

⏱️ Approach Speed Control

Too fast and you miss the hurdle timing; too slow and you lack height. Finding your optimal speed is key! The rhythm of your last three steps matters most.

🤲 Contact Timing

Place your hands on the far end of the vault at the moment your body rises after the hurdle. Too early causes your body to buckle; too late and you miss the contact entirely.

📹 Video Feedback

Have a friend or teacher record your attempts so you can objectively review your form. Check your approach, flight posture, and landing section by section!

Safety Rules

Staying Safe

Vault involves landing from height — safety rules must always be followed!

🧱 Mats Are Mandatory

Thick safety mats must be placed in front of, beside, and behind the vault. They are the most basic safeguard for absorbing impact from failed landings or falls.

🙋 Spotter Required

A spotter (teacher or classmate) must stand beside the vault at all times. They should be ready to support the gymnast immediately in case of a failed contact or unstable landing.

🤚 Watch Your Wrists

Significant force is applied to the wrists during contact. Always stretch your wrists thoroughly beforehand, and stop immediately if you feel pain. Wrist guards add extra safety.

🦴 Protect Your Neck

There is a risk of neck injury during the forward roll vault. Keep your chin tucked to your chest and your back rounded. Never push through neck pain!

⚠️ Never Exceed Your Level

Attempting a height beyond your ability can lead to serious accidents. Master each level completely before moving up by just one level.

✅ Preparation Checklist

Wear non-slip athletic shoes, empty your pockets, secure glasses, tie back long hair. Warm up thoroughly (at least 10 minutes) to loosen joints and muscles before starting!

Advanced Learning

Learning More About the Vault

Beyond the basics — the science and scoring behind the vault

  • 1
    Biomechanics — The horizontal kinetic energy from the approach run is converted into vertical energy at the springboard. That is why faster running speed means higher jumps!
  • 2
    Olympic Scoring — Scores are determined by the sum of Difficulty (D-score) and Execution (E-score). Key evaluation criteria include flight height, posture quality, and landing stability.
  • 3
    Korean Vault Success — Yeo Seo-jeong won a bronze medal in the vault at the 2020 Tokyo Olympics — the first ever Olympic medal for Korean women's artistic gymnastics!
  • 4
    Fitness Components — Vault is a comprehensive event requiring explosive power (takeoff), strength (contact/push-off), flexibility (flight posture), coordination (movement sequencing), and balance (landing).

Self-Assessment

Check Your Vault Skills

Review your performance and identify areas for improvement

🏃 Approach Run

Can you run at a consistent speed and hit the springboard at the right spot? Check whether the rhythm of your last three steps feels natural.

🦶 Hurdle

Can you bring both feet together and push off the Reuther board with power? If you hear a loud "thwack," you are doing it right.

🤲 Contact

Can you place both hands precisely on the far end of the vault and push off hard? Make sure your elbows do not bend!

🤸 Flight Posture

Can you spread your legs wide enough (straddle) or curl your body tightly (forward roll) in the air? Your aerial posture is the key to an elegant performance.

🧍 Landing Stability

Can you land softly with both feet together? If you do not fall backward or slip, you pass!

💪 Spirit of Challenge

Are you overcoming fear and challenging yourself one level at a time? Vault builds not only physical strength but mental resilience too.

Quiz

True / False Quiz

Let's check what you have learned!

  • Q1
    In vault, the hurdle should be performed with one foot. — False. The standard vault hurdle is a two-foot takeoff from the Reuther board.
  • Q2
    In a forward roll vault, you should tuck your chin to your chest to protect your neck. — True. Neck protection is the top priority; always tuck your chin and round your back.
  • Q3
    Vault practice should start at the highest level. — False. Always start at a low level (3–4) and only raise it one level at a time after achieving consistent success.
  • Q4
    At contact, you should place your hands on the far end of the vault and push off hard. — True. A strong push-off determines your flight height and distance.
  • Q5
    Yeo Seo-jeong won a gold medal in vault at the 2020 Tokyo Olympics. — False. She won a bronze medal, which was the first Olympic medal in Korean women's artistic gymnastics history.
