High Jump

Defying gravity — the science of the Fosbury Flop

Introduction

What Is High Jump?

A track and field event where you run up, take off from one foot, and clear a horizontal crossbar to jump as high as possible

  • 1
    Objective — Clear the crossbar without knocking it down! Your highest successful height becomes your final result.
  • 2
    Amazing fact — World-class athletes clear heights 20-30cm above their own stature. The center of mass passes under the bar while the body passes over it!
  • 3
    Venue — Consists of a fan-shaped approach area, two uprights holding the crossbar, and a high-density landing mat.
Track & field event Jumping (vertical) discipline Olympic official event A signature event for experiencing challenge and achievement
History

History of the High Jump

From the scissors jump to the Fosbury Flop — a history of technical innovation

  • 1800s
    The scissors era — Early high jumpers used the "scissors jump," running straight at the bar and crossing legs over it. Intuitive but limited in height.
  • 1968
    The Fosbury Flop revolution — At the Mexico City Olympics, American Dick Fosbury won gold by going over the bar back-first with the "Fosbury Flop." Every expert mocked it, but this technique changed world records.
  • 1993
    World record — Cuba's Javier Sotomayor cleared 2.45m, setting a world record that has stood for over 30 years.
  • 2021
    Tokyo Olympics shared gold — Italy's Tamberi and Qatar's Barshim finished with the same height and chose to share the gold medal in a touching moment!

Before Fosbury, the "straddle" (face-down clearance) was also popular. But after the flop proved its efficiency, virtually all athletes today use the Fosbury Flop.

Rules

Competition Rules

Core rules for fair and safe competition

  • 1
    Bar height progression — The crossbar is raised incrementally, typically by 2-5cm. Starting height is set by the organizer.
  • 2
    3 consecutive failures = elimination — Three failures at the same height or across consecutive heights means elimination from the competition. Save your attempts wisely!
  • 3
    Tiebreaker — If final cleared heights are equal, the athlete with fewer failures at that height ranks higher. If still tied, total failures across all heights decide.
  • 4
    One-foot takeoff required — Jumping with both feet simultaneously is a disqualification! You must take off from one foot.
  • 5
    No passing under the bar — If any body part passes under the bar or outside the uprights, it counts as a failure.

Key Skills 1

Approach and Takeoff

The critical phase of converting horizontal speed into vertical height

  • 1
    J-curve approach — Run straight initially, then curve toward the bar in the last 3-5 steps, tracing the shape of the letter J. This curve is the secret of the Fosbury Flop! Centrifugal force naturally rotates the body.
  • 2
    Outside foot takeoff — Push off powerfully with the foot farther from the bar (outside foot). Approaching from the right? Left foot takes off. From the left? Right foot!
  • 3
    Speed → height conversion — In the last 2 steps, adjust stride to straighten the body. At takeoff, drive both arms and the opposite knee upward to maximize vertical lift.
  • 4
    Rhythm — The approach is typically 8-12 steps. Run with an accelerating rhythm: "ta-ta-ta, ta-ta-ta, TAK!"
Key Skills 2

Fosbury Flop

The scientific technique of clearing the bar with your back

  • 1
    Back to the bar — After takeoff, rotate 180° in the air so your back faces the bar. You clear it looking at the sky!
  • 2
    Back arch (bridge) — Create a bow-like arch with your lower back over the bar. This arch allows your center of mass to pass under the bar while your body passes over it!
  • 3
    Clearance sequence — Head → shoulders → back → hips → legs pass over the bar in order. Each part must clear sequentially to avoid contact.
  • 4
    Hip lift — As the hips pass the bar, engage your core to push hips upward while quickly tucking your legs up. This is the most common failure point!

The science of the Fosbury Flop: By arching the body, the center of mass (balance point of body weight) sits outside the body (below the bar). The body clears the bar even without lifting the entire mass above bar height!

Key Skills 3

Landing

Completing a successful attempt — safety comes first

  • 1
    Mat landing — High jump must always be performed onto a dedicated high-density mat. Never practice without a mat!
  • 2
    Back/shoulder landing — After the Fosbury Flop, the back and shoulders naturally contact the mat first. Spread arms to the sides to distribute impact.
  • 3
    Neck protection — Tuck your chin to your chest to prevent the neck from bending backward. Neck injuries are very serious, so always consciously tuck your chin!
  • 4
    Don't roll off — Be careful not to roll off the mat after landing. Stabilize your position after landing and get up slowly.
Tactics

Competition Tactics

High jump is a strategic battle as much as a technical one

📏 Starting Height Selection

Starting below your ability provides confidence but wastes energy. If confident, start higher to conserve your stamina!

⏭️ Pass Strategy

You can skip a height and attempt the next one. Passing easy heights reduces your attempt count, advantageous in tiebreakers. But it carries failure risk, so be careful.

🔋 Energy Management

High jump competitions can last 2+ hours. Stretch, hydrate, and maintain body temperature between attempts.

🧠 Mental Management

Fear grows as the bar rises. Use mental imagery of successful clearances, consistent routines, and deep breathing to maintain focus.

Safety Guidelines

Practicing Safely

High jump requires proper safety equipment and technique

🛏️ Mat Check Required

Always check mat position, thickness, and condition before practice. If the mat is separated from the uprights or has gaps between sections, do not jump! The mat must extend well beyond both sides of the bar.

🤸 Practice Landing First

Before attempting the Fosbury Flop, start with standing backward falls onto the mat. Repeat tucking your chin and landing on back/shoulders until it becomes natural.

📏 Check Bar Material

The crossbar should be lightweight and flexible (fiberglass, aluminum). Heavy wooden or metal bars can cause injuries when they fall — absolutely prohibited!

👤 One at a Time

Wait until one person has completely landed and stepped off the mat before the next person attempts. Jumping onto a mat with someone on it is extremely dangerous!

School PE

School PE Tips

Learn high jump safely and enjoyably

  • 1
    Step-by-step learning — (1) Standing backward falls (landing practice) → (2) Scissors jump (basic jumping) → (3) Straight approach flop → (4) J-curve approach flop.
  • 2
    Use elastic bands — Start with an elastic band instead of a crossbar. It is safe if touched and reduces fear.
  • 3
    Start at low heights — Begin at waist height to build successful experiences. Confidence is the key to improvement!
  • 4
    Personal challenge records — Don't compare with others; focus on beating your own previous record. High jump is a challenge against yourself.

Sports Science

The Science of High Jump

The physics principles hidden in the Fosbury Flop

  • 1
    The secret of center of mass — By arching the body in the Fosbury Flop, the center of mass sits outside the body (below the back). The center of mass passes under the bar, but each body part passes over it!
  • 2
    Kinetic → potential energy — The horizontal kinetic energy from the approach (1/2mv²) converts to vertical potential energy (mgh) at takeoff. The faster you run, the higher you can jump!
  • 3
    Centrifugal force and the J-curve — The centrifugal force generated during the curved approach naturally tilts the body toward the bar at takeoff and assists with mid-air rotation.
  • 4
    Conservation of angular momentum — Moving arms and legs in the air adjusts rotation speed and posture. The same principle as a figure skater spinning faster when they pull in their arms!

Where PE meets science! Understanding the physics principles of high jump and embodying them physically is key to improving.

True/False Quiz

  • Q1
    In high jump, you can take off with both feet simultaneously. — False. You must take off from one foot; a two-foot jump is disqualification.
  • Q2
    Three consecutive failures at the same height means elimination. — True. Three failures at the same height or across consecutive heights means competition elimination.
  • Q3
    In the Fosbury Flop, the center of mass passes over the bar. — False. By arching the body, the center of mass passes under the bar while the body passes over it.
  • Q4
    The approach follows a J-shaped curve. — True. Start straight, then curve toward the bar in the last 3-5 steps, utilizing centrifugal force.
  • Q5
    The high jump world record of 2.45m was set in 2000. — False. It was set by Cuba's Sotomayor in 1993 and has remained unbroken for over 30 years.
