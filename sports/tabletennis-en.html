Introduction

What is Table Tennis?

A racket sport where players hit a small ball back and forth across a table

  • 1
    Net Competition Sport — A net is set at the center of a table; players hit the ball with paddles. Score when the ball bounces twice on the opponent's side or the opponent fails to return.
  • 2
    Olympic Sport — Official since the 1988 Seoul Olympics. Features men's/women's singles, team events, and mixed doubles.
  • 3
    World's Most Popular Racket Sport — The most widely played racket sport globally. Hundreds of millions play, centered on China across Asia and Europe.
  • 4
    Korea is a Table Tennis Powerhouse — A traditional powerhouse with Olympic gold medalists like Yoo Nam-kyu and Hyun Jung-hwa. Recently, Shin Yu-bin has been leading Korean table tennis on the world stage.

History

History of Table Tennis

From an English dinner table game to an Olympic sport

  • 1
    1880s England — Upper-Class Parlor Game — In rainy England, people wanted to play tennis indoors. They used books as nets and cork stoppers as balls on a dinner table. The name 'Table Tennis' originates here.
  • 2
    Celluloid Ball (1900s) — The introduction of celluloid balls standardized the bounce, greatly improving the game. The nickname 'Ping-Pong' also appeared around this time.
  • 3
    1926: ITTF Founded & 1st World Championships — The International Table Tennis Federation was established in London. The same year, the first World Table Tennis Championships were held.
  • 4
    1988 Seoul Olympics — Table tennis became an Olympic sport at the Seoul Games. Korea's Yoo Nam-kyu won the men's singles gold.
  • 5
    1991 Unified Korean Team — At the Chiba World Championships, North and South Korea formed a unified team and won the women's team event! A historic moment of reconciliation through sports.
  • 6
    Korean Olympic Medal History — Yoo Nam-kyu (1988 gold), Hyun Jung-hwa (1992 gold), Ryu Seung-min (2004 gold), and Shin Yu-bin (2024 Paris, mixed doubles bronze) continue the tradition.
Equipment · Facilities

Equipment and Court

The equipment and court specifications for table tennis

  • 📐
    Table — 2.74m long, 1.525m wide, 76cm high. Dark green or blue matte surface with white line markings.
  • 🥅
    Net — 15.25cm high, clamped at both ends. Total net length is 1.83m (extends 15.25cm beyond each side).
  • 🏓
    Paddle — Two types: shakehand (handshake grip) and penhold (pen grip). One side must be red rubber, the other black.
  • ⚪
    Ball — 40mm diameter, 2.7g weight, ABS (plastic) material. Changed from celluloid for safety and durability.
  • 🏟️
    Playing Area — Minimum 14m x 7m (international), 8m x 4m (general). Non-slip floor, glare-free lighting.
Rules · Match

Match Rules

Best of 5 games, first to 11 points wins a game

🏆 Best of 5 Games

A match has 5 games; win 3 to win. Some events use best of 7 (win 4).

🔢 11 Points to Win

Each game goes to 11 points. Starting from 0, the rally winner gets 1 point.

⚖️ Deuce (10:10)

At 10-10, play continues until there's a 2-point lead. E.g., 12-10, 13-11. No point limit.

🔄 Serve Alternation

Serves alternate every 2 points. In deuce (after 10-10), alternate every 1 point.

📏 Key Serve Rules

Place ball on open palm, show it to opponent, toss 16cm+ vertically, and hit as it descends. Hiding the ball = fault!

🔁 Let

If a serve touches the net top and lands correctly on the opponent's side, it's a let (void) — re-serve with no score change.

👥 Doubles Rules

Serves must go diagonally. Partners must alternate hits; same player hitting twice = loss of point.

🔀 End Change

Switch ends after each game. In the final game, switch when one side reaches 5 points.

Rules · Serve

Detailed Serve Rules

Strict rules apply to table tennis serves

  • 1
    Show Ball on Open Palm — Before serving, place the ball on your flat, open free-hand palm and show it clearly to the opponent and umpire. Hiding = fault.
  • 2
    16cm+ Vertical Toss — Toss the ball nearly vertically 16cm or higher. Tossing sideways or too low = fault. No spin during the toss.
  • 3
    Hit During Descent — Hit the ball as it falls after the peak. Hitting on the way up = fault! This ensures serve fairness.
  • 4
    Your Side → Opponent's Side — The serve must bounce on your side first, then cross the net and bounce on the opponent's side. In singles, anywhere is valid.
  • 5
    Doubles: Diagonal Serve — In doubles, serve from your right half to the opponent's right half (diagonally). This is why the center line exists!
  • 6
    Serve Deception (Spin Variation) — From the same toss motion, vary the spin (topspin, backspin, sidespin, no-spin) — that's expert-level serve tactics.
Remember: The serve is the only chance to attack without interference! Vary your spin to keep opponents guessing.
Core Skill 1

Serve

The start of offense — master serve techniques

  • 1
    Forehand Short Serve — A short serve that bounces twice on the opponent's side. Prevents strong attacks. The most basic serve.
  • 2
    Backspin Serve — Scrape under the ball for underspin. Opponent's normal return will hit the net. A safe defensive serve.
  • 3
    Sidespin Serve — Scrape the side of the ball for sidespin. Deflects unpredictably off the opponent's paddle.
  • 4
    Long Serve — Fast and deep to the end of the table. Catches the opponent off guard when they expect a short serve.
  • 5
    Serve = Start of Attack! — The serve is step 1 of a 3-ball attack (Serve → opponent's return → your attack). The goal is to set up an advantageous 3rd ball.
Core Skill 2

Spin

Spin is the heart of table tennis! Physics in a tiny ball

  • 1
    Topspin — Brush upward on top of the ball, making it dip quickly. The basic spin for drives. Bounces fast and forward.
  • 2
    Backspin — Scrape under the ball backward, making it float. The basic spin for chops/defense. Bounces low and wants to come back.
  • 3
    Sidespin — Scrape the side of the ball, making it curve left or right. Used mainly in serves. Hits the opponent's paddle unpredictably.
  • 4
    No-Spin — Intentionally spinless. When the opponent adjusts for spin that isn't there, they mistime their return.
  • 5
    Reading Spin — Watch the opponent's racket swing direction and contact angle. Upward swing = topspin; downward scrape = backspin. With practice, you can even read spin from the ball's logo rotation!
Core Skill 3

Rally Techniques

Dominate rallies with diverse offensive and defensive skills

  • 1
    Forehand Drive — The most fundamental attack. Apply topspin while hitting hard. Connect waist rotation and arm swing for power.
  • 2
    Backhand Drive — Attack balls coming to the backhand side. Quick wrist snap and elbow rotation. Modern table tennis increasingly emphasizes the backhand.
  • 3
    Chop (Defense) — Apply backspin to return the ball low over the net from far back. Forces opponent attack errors.
  • 4
    Smash — Slam high balls down hard. Focus on speed over spin. The finishing blow.
  • 5
    Lob — Lift the ball high with heavy topspin to buy time against smashes. The heavy spin makes timing tricky for opponents.
  • 6
    Block — Hold the paddle up to redirect fast drives back. Uses the opponent's power with minimal swing. Essential in fast rallies.
Tactics

Table Tennis Tactics

Skill isn't enough — win the mental battle!

🎯 Serve & 3rd Ball Attack

The most fundamental attack pattern. Serve (1st ball) forces a weak return, then attack the 3rd ball hard. Serve spin and placement are key!

↔️ Corner Attack (Side to Side)

Alternate attacks to the opponent's forehand and backhand sides. Wider movement breaks form and creates openings.

⏩ Pace Variation

After a fast drive, suddenly drop short, or follow a slow ball with a fast smash. Speed changes disrupt timing.

🌀 Spin Variation

Same swing motion, different spin. Looks like topspin but is backspin; looks like sidespin but is no-spin. Forces judgment errors.

🎪 Target the Backhand

Most players' forehand is stronger. Concentrate on the backhand for weaker returns and attack opportunities.

🏃 Move Them All Directions

Beyond side-to-side, mix short balls (near net) and deep balls (endline) to move opponents front-to-back too. 3D placement breaks stamina and form.

Basics · Stance

Grip and Basic Stance

Correct grip and stance are the starting point of all techniques

🤝 Shakehand Grip

Hold the paddle as if shaking hands. The mainstream modern grip; quick forehand-backhand transitions. Balanced play using both sides.

✏️ Penhold Grip

Hold the paddle like a pen. An Asian traditional grip with great wrist flexibility, strong for serves and forehand attacks. Korean legends Yoo Nam-kyu and Ryu Seung-min used this. The modern 'reverse penhold backhand' is popular.

🧍 Ready Position

Slightly bent knees, upper body leaning forward. Feet shoulder-width apart, weight on the balls of the feet. Paddle centered in front, ready for either side.

👣 Footwork

Table tennis footwork means small, quick steps. Small movements beat big strides for optimal hitting position. Use side steps, pivots, and cross steps as needed.

Grip tip: Shakehand is recommended for beginners! Easy forehand-backhand transitions and balanced skill development. Penhold suits serve/forehand specialists.
Safety Guidelines

Playing Safely

Safety tips for injury prevention and long-term enjoyment

🦴 Back Injury Risk

Prolonged bent posture and frequent waist rotation strain the back. Strengthen core muscles and always stretch before and after play.

💪 Elbow Injury Risk

Repetitive swinging stresses the elbow joint (similar to tennis elbow). Avoid overtraining and stretch elbows regularly.

🤚 Wrist Injury Risk

Heavy wrist snap use during serves and drives loads the wrist. Wrist guards and rotation stretches help.

🦶 Ankle Injury Risk

Quick lateral movements and sharp turns can twist ankles. Wear cushioned indoor shoes and do ankle strengthening exercises.

👁️ Eye Fatigue

Tracking a tiny fast ball strains the eyes. After long play, look at distant objects to rest your eyes.

🤸 Stretching Required!

Stretch back, shoulders, elbows, wrists, knees, ankles in order. Wrist and back stretches are essential before table tennis!

Fun Facts

Table Tennis Trivia

How much do you know about table tennis?

  • Q1
    How much does a table tennis ball weigh? — Answer: 2.7g! This tiny 40mm ball can spin up to 150 rotations per second.
  • Q2
    Why are paddle sides different colors? — Because different rubber types can be applied to each side! Rules require one side red, one black, so opponents can tell which side was used.
  • Q3
    What is 'Ping Pong Diplomacy'? — The 1971 event where the US and China restored diplomatic relations through table tennis exchanges. A tiny ball broke down Cold War barriers!
  • Q4
    How fast is a pro's drive? — Up to 150+ km/h! Crosses the 2.74m table in about 0.06 seconds. Too fast to react by sight — prediction is key.
  • Q5
    Why did celluloid balls disappear? — Celluloid is flammable, making bulk transport dangerous. Since 2014, all balls switched to safe ABS (poly) material.
Fun fact: Table tennis is called a 'brain sport.' In 0.5 seconds, you must read the spin, decide paddle angle, and choose placement!
Quiz

True/False Quiz

Let's check what you've learned!

  • Q1
    Table tennis matches are best of 3 games to 11 points. — False. International standard is best of 5 (or 7). School PE may use best of 3.
  • Q2
    When serving, the ball must be tossed 16cm+ vertically from an open palm. — True. Hiding the ball or adding spin to the toss is a fault.
  • Q3
    The table tennis ball diameter is about 40mm. — True. Changed from 38mm to 40mm after 2000.
  • Q4
    If a serve hits the net and goes over, it's a lost point. — False. If it hits the net and lands correctly, it's a 'let' — re-serve with no score change.
  • Q5
    Table tennis became an Olympic sport at the 1988 Seoul Olympics. — True. Korea's Yoo Nam-kyu won the men's singles gold at that inaugural event.
